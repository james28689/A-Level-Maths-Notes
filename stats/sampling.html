Sampling Techniques

Types of Data

  • Data can be divided into two main types: qualitative and quantitative.
  • Qualitative data is non numerical, e.g: written opinions on the state of roads.
  • Quantitative data is numerical, e.g: the number of different colour cars that pass a certain point.

Discrete, Continuous, and Categoric Data

  • Quantitative data can be divided into 3 types:
    1. Continuous data is data that is numerical in nature and can take an infinite number of values.
    2. Discrete data is data that can only take certain values, and and these values are countable.
    3. Categoric data is where the data is discrete but the variables are non-numeric.

Populations and Samples

  • A population is the entire set of objects from which we can draw a sample.
  • A sample is a subset of the population. When a sample is the same size as its population, it becomes a census.
  • The advantages of taking a census rather than a sample are:
    • You get data about the characteristics of the entire population.
  • The disadvantages are:
    • Costly
    • Time consuming
  • Taking a sample mitigates these disadvantages provided the sample is representative of the population. A sample that does not represent its population is referred to as biased.

Sampling Techniques

  • Simple Random Sampling

  • Stratified Sampling

  • This is a sampling technique in which every subcategory of the population is proportionally represented within the sample.
  • Once the sizes of subcategories aer chosen for the sample, use simple random sampling on each subcategory.

  • Systematic Sampling

  • This involves coming up with a rule to apply to a population to generate your sample, e.g: choosing every 10th person from a list of the entire population.

NOTE: All of the above methods assume that any participants are willing. This is rarely the case, in such circumstances where willing participants are scarce, the following methods could be used.

  • Opportunity Sampling

  • Ask the first people you see until you have enough data.

  • Quota Sampling

  • Have an idea of how many of each subcategory of the population are to be surveyed (e.g: as in stratified) then use opportunity sampling to fill the quota.

  • Cluster Sampling

  • Choose a cluster of people from a population then ask them all.
